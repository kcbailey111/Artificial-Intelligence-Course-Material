Section A — Logic and Search 
Sample 1. Iterative deepening depth -first search (IDS) is often described as combining the best properties of depth -first 
and breadth-first search. A student argues that “IDS is strictly better than BFS in every case since it uses less memory and 
finds optimal solutions. Hence, there is no reason to ever prefer BFS.” Evaluate this claim. 
Your answer must address: 
• What property of a problem IDS relies on to guarantee optimality, and under what conditions that property fails; 
• A concrete scenario where BFS finds an optimal solution but IDS does not; and 
• What this reveals about how the choice of search algorithm should be driven by problem structure rather than 
general-purpose efficiency claims. 
 
Section B — Reinforcement Learning and Language Models 
Sample 2. A language model is evaluated using cross -entropy loss on a held -out test set. A researcher observes that after 
an additional week of training, the model's cross -entropy on the test set increases slightly, even though its cross -entropy 
on the training set continues to decrease. The researcher concludes the model has begun to overfit and immediately halts 
training. Evaluate this conclusion. 
Your answer must address: 
• What cross-entropy measures in the context of language modeling and how it relates to perplexity; 
• Why a divergence between training and test cross -entropy is not by itself sufficient evidence of harmful 
overfitting; and 
• A concrete downstream task where a model at the earlier checkpoint might actually perform worse despite having 
lower test cross-entropy. 
 
Section C — Machine Learning 
Sample 3. CNNs and GNNs both update a node's (or pixel's) representation by aggregating information from its local 
neighborhood, yet they make fundamentally different assumptions about the structure of their input. A colleague proposes 
using a CNN to classify molecules by their biological activity, reasoning that “a molecule is just a graph, and a graph can 
be represented as a matrix, so a CNN should work fine.” Evaluate this proposal. 
Your answer must address: 
• What structural assumption a CNN makes about its input that allows neighborhood aggregation to work 
effectively, and why that assumption holds for images but fails for molecular graphs; 
• What property of GNN message passing makes it better suited to graph-structured data, and what it preserves that 
a CNN applied to an adjacency matrix would discard; and 
• One genuine limitation that GNNs share with CNNs despite their architectural differences, grounded in what both 
approaches ultimately do when they collapse neighborhood information into a single representation.
