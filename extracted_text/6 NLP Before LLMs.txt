What is NLP?
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field at the intersection 
of computer science, artificial intelligence, and linguistics that 
focuses on enabling computers to understand, interpret, 
generate, and respond to human language in a meaningful way.

language like a first-order predicate calculus.
Knowledge is stored as natural language and not in a formal

Historical Approaches to NLP
1950s-1980s
Symbolic/Rule-Based
• Hand-crafted grammars and rules
• Parse trees, formal grammars, logic-based reasoning
• Limitation: Limited coverage, brittle (breaks on unexpected input)
• Required enormous manual effort to handle linguistic variation
1990s-2010s
Statistical/Probabilistic
2010s-Present
Neural Networks
• Deep learning: learn multiple levels of representation automatically
• Word embeddings (2013), Recurrent Neural Networks, Transformers (2017)
• Modern Large Language Models: GPT, BERT, Claude, ChatGPT
• Breakthrough: Can learn complex patterns from massive amounts of text
• Requires large datasets and computational resources
• Learn patterns from data rather than hand-code rules
• Use probability theory to handle uncertainty and variation
• N-gram models, Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), 
Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars (PCFGs)
• Advantage: Robust, handles unseen cases better

quality scores)
Example: Compare language models by their

Method: Evaluate language model in isolation on 
held-out test data
Measure: Perplexity—how well the model predicts 
test sequences
Example: Compute perplexity on test corpus, lower 
is better
• Directly meaningful - measures what we 
care about
• Task-relevant and realistic
• Gold standard for model selection
• Expensive: requires full application pipeline
• Slow: each evaluation takes significant time
• Confounded: improvements could be from 
other system components, not the language 
model
Positives
Negatives
• May not correlate with downstream 
performance
• Lower perplexity ≠ better application results
• Disconnected from real end-to-end goals
• Fast: no need to build full application
• Easy to compare: single numeric metric

Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) is a practical algorithm for learning 
word embeddings that implements the Skip-gram objective with 
computational optimizations. 
Core Algorithm: Skip-Gram
• Given center word → predict context words
• Learns embeddings 𝐯w that capture distributional semantics
Possible Optimizations (These make training 
feasible on large corpora (billions of words):
• Negative Sampling: Instead of full softmax 
over 𝑉  words, sample a few "negative" 
examples (~5-20) (words not in the context).
• Subsampling: Down-weight very frequent 
words ("the", "a", "is")
Word2Vec was revolutionary:
• Simple, efficient, and scalable
• Produced high-quality embeddings that capture semantic and syntactic relationships
• Made word embeddings accessible and practical for NLP applications
• Famous for capturing analogies: king - man + woman ≈ queen
Pre-trained embeddings:
• Can download Word2Vec embeddings trained on Google News (3 billion words)
• Use immediately without training: plug-and-play for downstream tasks

• Idioms like “kick the bucket” ≠ sum

Summary
1. Language Models: Assign probabilities to word sequences
2. N-grams: Use Markov assumption to make estimation tractable
3. Smoothing: Handle unseen events (critical for larger n)
4. Perplexity: Intrinsic evaluation metric (lower is better)
5. Word Embeddings: Dense vectors capture semantic similarity
6. Distributional Semantics: “You shall know a word by the company it keeps”
• Larger 𝑛 → better modeling, but more sparsity
• Higher dimensions → more expressivity, but harder to train
• Simple methods (𝑛-grams) still useful in many applications
